# Learning Log 2026-W23

## Role

R10 QA & Learning Log Lead

## Purpose

This file records what the team learned from comparing last week's prediction with this week's actual market outcome.

The focus is on prediction quality, evidence weighting, LLM performance, and how our market analysis can improve next sprint.

---

## Actual Market Outcome

The actual market result for Week 23 was bearish.

Key market results:

- SPX / S&P 500 fell about -2.57% to -2.64%.
- NDX / Nasdaq 100 fell about -4.53%.
- IWM / Russell 2000 fell about -3.06%.
- VIX rose, showing higher market fear.
- Technology was the weakest major sector.
- Defensive sectors performed better than growth and cyclical sectors.
- Bitcoin fell sharply, showing weak speculative risk appetite.

Overall, the market shifted into a risk-off environment.

---

## What We Learned

This week showed that short-term bullish technical signals can fail quickly when macro risk, seasonal weakness, and volatility pressure become stronger.

Last week, several models focused heavily on the fact that SPX, NDX, and IWM were above their 8 EMA and 21 EMA. However, the actual market result showed that this bullish technical evidence was not strong enough to overcome bearish June seasonality, rising risk signals, and sector weakness.

We learned that a good prediction should not only identify the strongest evidence, but also correctly judge which evidence matters most for the coming week.

---

## What Worked Well

- The team saved four LLM raw responses, which made it possible to compare model performance after the actual result.
- The R6 comparison helped show clear differences between ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and DeepSeek.
- The prediction process included multiple evidence types: Almanac, Macro / News, Technical, and market data.
- DeepSeek provided a more cautious view, which helped reveal that the bullish view was not the only possible interpretation.
- The calibration process helped us identify that the bullish predictions were too optimistic.

---

## What Did Not Work Well

- The prediction leaned too bullish compared with the actual bearish result.
- The analysis gave too much weight to short-term EMA strength.
- The team underestimated bearish June midterm-year seasonality.
- The team did not give enough weight to VIX rising and risk-off signals.
- Technology was expected to remain strong, but it became the weakest major sector.
- NDX was expected to be the strongest index by several models, but it became the weakest.
- The bearish contradiction evidence was identified, but it was not weighted strongly enough in the final interpretation.

---

## LLM Performance Lesson

The four AI models did not perform equally.

ChatGPT and Gemini were too bullish. They focused strongly on technical momentum and technology leadership.

Claude was more cautious, but still leaned neutral-to-bullish.

DeepSeek performed best because it gave the most cautious and downside-aware view. Although it was not bearish enough, it was closer to the actual market outcome than the other models.

This suggests that the team should not simply follow the majority AI view. A minority or more cautious model may be useful when the market contains strong contradiction signals.

---

## Evidence Weighting Lesson

This week showed that not all evidence should be weighted equally.

The team should give more weight to bearish evidence when several warning signals appear together, such as:

- Weak June midterm-year Almanac pattern
- Rising VIX
- Technology sector weakness
- Stronger dollar
- Geopolitical risk
- Defensive sector outperformance

If these signals appear together, the team should reduce bullish confidence even if charts still look strong.

---

## Improvement for Next Sprint

Next sprint, the team should improve the prediction process by:

- Comparing bullish and bearish evidence more evenly.
- Giving stronger weight to contradiction signals.
- Treating VIX and sector rotation as major risk indicators.
- Avoiding overreliance on EMA signals.
- Checking whether Technology leadership is continuing or reversing.
- Paying more attention to the most cautious AI model, not only the majority view.
- Reducing confidence when evidence is mixed or when seasonal and macro risks conflict with technical momentum.

---

## Key Takeaway

The main lesson from Week 23 is that bullish momentum can reverse quickly when macro, seasonal, and volatility risks align.

For the next sprint, the team should focus less on simply identifying whether evidence is bullish or bearish, and more on deciding which evidence deserves the most weight.

This will help improve calibration and reduce the risk of overconfident or overly optimistic predictions.
